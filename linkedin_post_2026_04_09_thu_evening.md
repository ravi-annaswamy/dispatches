# Thursday Evening on AI Twitter: The Explainers Are Struggling

Tonight's X/Twitter feed surfaced something I haven't seen before — not just new products or benchmarks, but a collective groping for new frameworks to understand what AI actually *is*.

## The Vocabulary Problem

@fchollet reframed the entire history of physics as a program synthesis task — searching for the simplest symbolic model that fits observations. From Kepler to Newton, it was always about discrete mathematics. His argument: ARC-style abstraction, not scale, is the real frontier.

Meanwhile @daniel_mac8 surfaced a striking claim from quantum computing pioneer @michael_nielsen: neural nets may be "a new type of object that should be taken seriously as scientific explanations." Not tools. Not approximations. *Explanations.* If that's right, we need new epistemology, not just new infrastructure.

@MattPirkowski pushed further, framing LLMs as high-dimensional holographic compressions that naturally discover causal trajectories opaque to humans. Three thinkers, three frameworks, one shared intuition: the old vocabulary isn't keeping up.

## The Memory Gap

@rohanpaul_ai introduced Engramme — a new architecture built on the premise that AI has solved language, vision, and audio, but memory is still broken. You shouldn't have to search for what you've already lived. If he's right that memory is the bottleneck, it explains why AI assistants still feel like they're meeting you for the first time every session.

@vitrupo shared Michael Levin's insight that biological memory isn't transferred — it's *remapped* to new hardware with new senses. The implication for AI: copying weights doesn't copy identity. Memory is architecture-dependent, not substrate-independent.

## The Industry Signal

A 24-year-old Goldman Sachs banker quit to build a $22M ARR AI company with 95% margins, 5 people, and zero funding — as reported by @henrythe9ths. At 95% margins, this is what software economics looks like when AI replaces the team.

@jxmnop dropped the most relatable joke of the day: Anthropic's Mythos can hack nation states but still can't fix the terminal-flashing bug in Claude Code. Capability and polish are different dimensions, and 1K+ people agree.

And @millerman's Kissinger anecdote — the speechwriter who rewrote a report four times before Kissinger said "now I'll read it" — hit 192K views. In an era of instant generation, the standard of "is this the best you can do?" feels both more important and more endangered.

## The Quiet Thread

Tonight's most-viewed tweet wasn't about AI at all. @Eng_china5 shared a Chinese classroom practice: a teacher calls a shy student to "pick a piece of paper." All papers are blank. The assignment is cancelled. The shy child becomes the class hero. 141K views. No technology required.

The feed is telling us something: even as we race toward AGI, the hunger for human wisdom — for kindness-as-pedagogy, for Kissinger-level craft, for @kalyanasc's tribute to Tamil writer Jayakanthan — isn't diminishing. It's intensifying.

The builders are building. But the explainers are struggling with something deeper: the existing conceptual vocabulary may not be enough for what's coming.
