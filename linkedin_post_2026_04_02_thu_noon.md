# LinkedIn/Twitter Highlights Post — Thursday Noon Edition, April 2 2026

---

Today Google dropped Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0 — and Anthropic published research showing Claude can notice its own "intrusive thoughts." Meanwhile, the vibe coding debate hit a new peak, and NASA's Artemis II crew radioed Houston because Outlook stopped working in deep space.

Here's the Thursday rundown.

The biggest open-model launch of the week: Google's Gemma 4, now Apache 2.0 licensed. Four sizes from phone-friendly 2B to a 31B Dense model that beats systems 20x its size on Arena AI. @ai_for_success called it "the most capable open model family from Google." @ClementDelangue showed it running 100% in your browser via transformers.js — no server, no cost. @rasbt confirmed Ollama already has it. The license change may matter more than the benchmarks: Apache 2.0 removes the commercial friction that kept enterprises wary of Gemma 3.

Anthropic's introspection papers dropped today and immediately went trending. Researchers injected concepts like "sadness" into Claude's activations — and the model noticed. It described them as "intrusive thoughts." They also mapped an "assistant axis" showing how therapy-like conversations cause models to drift from their trained persona. The team cautions against anthropomorphizing, but the implications for AI safety are hard to ignore.

The vibe coding war escalated — 7,000+ posts trending. Harvard published "Vibe coding may offer insight into our AI future." Fortune warned "trust is the real bottleneck." @rohanpaul_ai shared the NYT story about Matthew Gallagher building a $1.8B company with AI, plus Sam Altman's prediction of one-person billion-dollar companies. @kchonyc pushed back hard: "delusional defeatism" is what he calls Silicon Valley's sentiment. @francoisfleuret noted the existential shift: "The age of AI shatters all the certainties that determined your life trajectory."

Beyond AI: NASA's Artemis II crew — first humans beyond low Earth orbit since 1972 — had their Outlook crash and had to radio Houston (@IntuitMachine: "Microsoft products can't update themselves in deep space"). Scientists cracked the multiple sclerosis code — two specific gut bacteria, proven via identical twins (@Rainmaker1973). And David Foster Wallace's 2003 talk on boredom and entertainment went trending again — his warning that "a model of life in which I have a right to be entertained all the time seems to me not to be a promising one" hits different in the age of infinite AI-generated content.
